And hyperlinking a few loose pages or even pagination in the traditional sense isn't the same thing. Spine restricts the arrangement on the stack and makes for linear reading experience. I found it particularly nice to read piecemealed content on mobile and tablets with page-flip. The spine broke down the content into small chewable chunks that were easier to remember.

About ten years ago people tried emulating the book with third-party plugins like Macromedia Flash, but those attempts were crude. The experience far from ideal, especially, when web was desktop-only. In my opinion flash undid the cause of books more than anything else. It left a bad aftertaste, so much so that many of us chose to ripping apart our books at the spine and turning them into websites. Ending up here, with a bad choice of design and often poor relationship with book lovers on the web.

I wish web had better support for books with a spine_url to host and thread pages on. A tag that considered all semantics and pragmatics of a book and its subresources. Like pages, cover, colophon etc. Only then could web catch up with what's truly a book reading experience. Go beyond the scroll, but that's a discussion for another place and time.
